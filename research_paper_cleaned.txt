Title:
From Confusion to Clarity: IT Undergraduates, Industry Expectations, and the Role of Quality Internships (2024–2026)

1. Introduction

Many IT undergraduates feel confused about career paths because the IT industry is broad, fast-changing, and poorly mapped onto university curricula.  Between 2024 and 2026, roles in AI, data science, cloud, and cybersecurity have grown rapidly, but universities still emphasize traditional programming and theory, leaving students unsure about which roles fit them and what skills they actually need. techsciresearch [1]

This gap is now widely documented:
- Around 80–90% of employers report significant IT skills gaps, especially in AI, cloud, and cybersecurity. dl.acm [2]
- Recent studies show that graduates are technically literate but often lack data-handling, cloud-native tools, and real-world workflow experience. files.eric.ed [3]

At the same time, high-quality internships have been shown to strongly boost career confidence and clarity, while low-quality ones leave students as uncertain as before.  This paper links these three threads: formosa [4]
1. Why IT undergraduates feel confused.
2. How universities can better align IT training with industry needs.
3. How quality internships help students translate academic learning into real-world confidence.

2. Why IT Undergraduates Feel Confused

Students' confusion typically comes from three sources: finalroundai [5]

1. Too many choices, too little guidance
   - IT today includes AI, data science, cloud, DevOps, cybersecurity, UI/UX, QA, blockchain, game dev, IoT, and more. techsciresearch [1]
   - Many universities do not guide students to choose and focus on one track early. ncas.ac [6]

2. Mismatch between theory and practice
   - Courses cover algorithms, databases, and software engineering well, but often miss:
     - Data science and analytics workflows.
     - Cloud platforms (AWS/Azure/GCP), containers, CI/CD.
     - Security-focused or DevOps thinking. journals.sagepub [7]
   - As a result, students know "how to code" but not "how code is actually shipped and maintained in a company." dl.acm [2]

3. Soft-skills and career-awareness gap
   - Employers value communication, teamwork, problem-solving, and business awareness, which are often under-developed compared with technical skills. jisem-journal [8]
   - Many students do not know how to:
     - Talk about their projects in interviews.
     - Explain which roles (e.g., AI engineer vs. data analyst) fit them. files.eric.ed [9]

3. Major IT Career Paths (Simplified)

Below are key IT fields undergraduates encounter, with simple explanations and core skills. pluralsight [10]

Major fields, typical roles, and key skills:
- AI / ML: Build models that predict or decide (e.g., chatbots, recommendations).  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Junior ML Engineer, AI Engineer. Key skills: Python, TensorFlow/PyTorch, stats, SQL; problem-solving, communication.
- Data Science / Analytics: Turn data into business insights and dashboards.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Data Analyst, Junior Data Scientist. Key skills: Python/R, SQL, visualization (Power BI/Tableau); data storytelling.
- Software Engineering: Build and maintain web, mobile, or backend systems.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Junior Software Engineer, Full-stack Developer. Key skills: JavaScript/Python/Java, Git, databases; teamwork, debugging.
- Cybersecurity: Protect systems from attacks and detect threats.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Security Analyst, SOC Analyst. Key skills: Networking, Linux, scripting, SIEM tools; attention to detail.
- Cloud / DevOps: Run apps on cloud platforms and automate deployments.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Cloud Support, DevOps Associate. Key skills: Cloud (AWS/Azure/GCP), Docker/Kubernetes, CI/CD; systematic thinking.
- UI/UX Design: Design how apps and websites look and feel.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Junior UX/UI Designer. Key skills: Figma/Sketch, basic HTML/CSS; empathy, iteration.
- QA / Software Testing: Check that software works correctly before release.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: QA Engineer, Test Automation Engineer. Key skills: Testing tools, basic code; attention to detail.
- Networking / SysAdmin: Keep networks and servers running.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: IT Support, Junior Network Engineer. Key skills: Networking, Windows/Linux; customer service.
- Blockchain / Web3: Build decentralized apps and smart contracts.  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Junior Blockchain Developer. Key skills: Solidity/Rust, Web3; risk-aware thinking.
- Game Development: Create video games (logic, graphics, online systems).  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Junior Game Developer, QA Tester (games). Key skills: C++/C#, Unity/Unreal; creativity, teamwork.
- Embedded / IoT: Program hardware devices (sensors, controllers).  finalroundai [5] Entry-level roles: Embedded Software Engineer, IoT Developer. Key skills: C/C++, microcontrollers; precision problem-solving.

Recent global salary trends show that AI/ML, cloud, and cybersecurity roles tend to have higher entry-level ranges (often \$75k–150k USD), while data science, QA, networking, and embedded roles sit in the \$50k–90k range, depending on country and company size. thisisanitsupportgroup [11]

4. Addressing the Gap Between Academic IT Training and Industry Needs

4.1. Where the gap lies

Studies consistently find that: journals.sagepub [12]

- IT curricula are strong in theory (programming, algorithms, software engineering) but weak in context-specific skills such as:
  - Data science and analytics.
  - Cloud platforms and DevOps.
  - Cybersecurity and compliance.
- Employers complain that graduates can solve textbook problems but struggle with real datasets, messy production code, and cross-team workflows. techsciresearch [1]
- Soft skills such as communication, project management, and business-awareness are often under-emphasized. evolllution [13]

For example, a 2025 UK study on AI showed that while universities teach ML theory well, they under-emphasize data handling, deployment, and real-world trade-offs that employers need. journals.sagepub [7]

4.2. What universities can do

Recent research on industry-driven curricula and skills-gap studies suggests several practical steps. inderscienceonline [14]

1. Industry-informed curriculum redesign
   - Include industry professionals on curriculum boards to update syllabi and labs regularly. inderscienceonline [14]
   - Use industry-specific "bodies of knowledge" (e.g., SFIA, e-CF) to standardize IT competencies and role profiles. files.eric.ed [3]

2. Project-based and problem-based learning
   - Replace or supplement theory-heavy lectures with capstone projects that mirror real-world tasks (e.g., a small web app with CI/CD, a data dashboard for a mock business, a security audit). peer.asee [15]

3. Short-term skill supplements and micro-credentials
   - Offer workshops and micro-courses on tools such as:
     - Cloud platforms (AWS/Azure/GCP). evolllution [13]
     - Docker, Kubernetes, CI/CD. evolllution [13]
     - Data tools (SQL, Python, Power BI/Tableau). files.eric.ed [3]
   - Run "conversion-style" bootcamps so students (even non-IT majors) can pivot into cybersecurity, cloud, or data roles. bcs [16]

4. Deep industry-academia partnerships
   - Co-design joint projects and internships where students solve real company problems. ncas.ac [6]
   - Establish feedback loops: companies report on graduate performance and suggest syllabus updates. ncas.ac [6]

5. Embed ethics and digital context
   - Add modules on AI ethics, privacy, sustainability, and green IT, so students understand how technology fits into business and society, not just into code. linkedin [17]

5. Impact of Internship Quality on Career Confidence

5.1. What "high-quality" means

High-quality IT internships are: conference.unklab.ac [18]

- Real-world tasks: Actual coding, data analysis, testing, or system support, not just admin work.
- Structured supervision: Regular feedback, clear goals, and a mentor who explains workflows.
- Aligned with learning outcomes: Tasks that build skills relevant to future roles (e.g., cloud, DevOps, data, security).

Low-quality internships are the opposite: unclear tasks, weak supervision, and little connection to real-world IT practice. files.eric.ed [9]

5.2. How quality internships boost confidence

Recent studies and institutional reports show that: frontiersin [19]

- Career clarity improves: One 2025 study found that 84% of students gained clearer career orientation after a well-structured IT internship, helping them decide between roles like data science, software engineering, or cybersecurity. files.eric.ed [9]
- Professional confidence grows: 89% of students reported enhanced professional confidence, feeling closer to "job-ready." files.eric.ed [9]
- Confidence in talking about skills rises: In the U.S., 80% of interns felt "very much" or "quite a bit" confident discussing their skills with employers, versus only 68% of non-interns. strada [20]
- Identity and motivation strengthen: A 2025 psychology study showed that higher-quality internships significantly strengthen professional identity, which in turn boosts career motivation and positive attitudes. frontiersin [19]
- An Indonesian IT-informatics study (2026) found that students with structured, real-world internships reported much higher satisfaction, career-planning readiness, and confidence in portfolio-building. formosa [4]

5.3. Mechanisms: Why quality matters

Key mechanisms linking quality internships to confidence include: frontiersin [21]

- Concrete proof of ability: Working on real projects (e.g., a deployed web app, a data pipeline, a security check) gives students evidence they can do real IT work, reducing self-doubt. frontiersin [21]
- Soft-skills growth: Collaboration, stand-up meetings, and code reviews improve communication and teamwork, which employers rate highly. jisem-journal [8]
- Mentorship and feedback: Supervised feedback helps students see strengths and gaps, building a more realistic, confident self-image. formosa [4]

6. Practical Roadmaps for Students (Beginner-Friendly)

Below are condensed learning paths that help students bridge the gap proactively. roadmap [22]

6.1. AI/ML Engineer

- Foundation (0–3 months): Python, NumPy, Pandas, basic stats (probability, regression). scribd [23]
- Machine Learning (4–6 months): Classic ML (linear regression, decision trees, random forests, XGBoost) and small projects (e.g., house-price prediction, customer-churn model). roadmap [22]
- Deep Learning (7–9 months): Neural networks, CNNs/RNNs, TensorFlow/PyTorch; build an image-classifier or text model. pluralsight [10]
- Deployment & MLOps (10–12 months): Docker, cloud (AWS/Azure/GCP), Flask/FastAPI APIs, basic MLOps (versioning, monitoring). dl.acm [2]
- Ongoing: Kaggle-style projects, open-source contributions, optional certifications (e.g., cloud ML or AI-engineer tracks). pluralsight [10]

6.2. Data Scientist

- Months 0–3: Python, SQL, basic statistics, Excel. linkedin [24]
- Months 4–6: Pandas, data-cleaning, simple visualizations (Matplotlib/Seaborn), basic hypothesis testing. linkedin [24]
- Months 7–9: Intro to ML (regression, classification), build a small predictive model. roadmap [22]
- Months 10–12: Learn a BI tool (Power BI/Tableau), create dashboards for mock business questions. techsciresearch [1]
- Ongoing: Portfolio with 3–5 end-to-end projects (data collection, cleaning, modeling, reporting). dl.acm [2]

6.3. Software Engineer (full-stack, web-oriented)

- Months 0–3: One language (JavaScript/Python/Java), algorithms basics, Git, GitHub. hayksimonyan.substack [25]
- Months 4–6: Frontend (HTML/CSS/JS or React/Vue) + backend (Node/Django/Spring) basics; build a simple CRUD app. finalroundai [5]
- Months 7–9: Databases (SQL or NoSQL), basic APIs, unit testing. hayksimonyan.substack [25]
- Months 10–12: Intro to cloud (deploy an app on a cloud platform) and CI/CD basics. hayksimonyan.substack [25]
- Ongoing: Contribute to open-source, maintain a GitHub profile with 3–5 meaningful projects. dl.acm [2]

6.4. Cybersecurity Analyst

- Months 0–3: Networking basics (TCP/IP, DNS, VPN), Linux, basic scripting (Python/Bash), security concepts (confidentiality, integrity, availability). pluralsight [26]
- Months 4–6: Learn a SIEM or log-analysis tool, run vulnerability scanners, understand common attack types (phishing, malware, DDoS). pluralsight [26]
- Months 7–9: Practice in labs (e.g., TryHackMe, Hack The Box), participate in security CTFs. bcs [16]
- Months 10–12: Consider entry-level certs (e.g., CompTIA Security+, cloud-security fundamentals). pluralsight [26]
- Ongoing: Build a small portfolio of lab write-ups, security reports, and automation scripts. bcs [16]

7. How Students Can Choose a Path

To reduce confusion, IT undergraduates can: thisisanitsupportgroup [11]

- Limit early breadth: Focus on one track (e.g., "Python-based data track" or "web-focused full-stack") for 1–2 years, then specialize further. pluralsight [10]
- Do 1–2 quality internships: Prefer internships with real tasks, a mentor, and clear learning outcomes; one high-quality IT internship is worth more than two shallow ones. formosa [4]
- Build a public portfolio: Use GitHub, a personal website, or a simple blog to show:
  - Code projects,
  - Data analyses,
  - Security labs or cloud deployments. techsciresearch [1]
- Use AI tools as assistants, not crutches: Use tools like ChatGPT or Copilot to speed up practice and documentation, but always check understanding and deepen knowledge on your own. sundaytimes [27]

8. Conclusion and Future Outlook 

The IT industry is evolving rapidly, and the gap between university education and industry expectations continues to grow. Many undergraduates remain confused because they are exposed to multiple fields without clear guidance on specialization.

However, this challenge also presents a major opportunity.

Students who:

Focus on one domain
Build real-world projects
Gain hands-on experience
Use modern tools and technologies

can successfully transition into the industry.

Looking forward (2024–2030):

AI, Cloud, and Cybersecurity will dominate
Automation will reduce low-skill jobs
High-skill, problem-solving roles will increase

Therefore, the key to success is:
  Not just learning — but building, practicing, and specializing

# 📄 **A Comparative Study of Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Generative AI Roles with Industry Trends (2026–2030)**

## **1. Introduction**

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is one of the fastest-growing fields in the modern IT industry. With rapid advancements in technology, new roles such as Machine Learning (ML) Engineer and Generative AI (Gen AI) Engineer have emerged.

* Which career path to choose

Artificial Intelligence is a broad field that focuses on creating systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence.

### **2.1 Responsibilities**

* Designing intelligent systems
* Building automation solutions
* Developing decision-making algorithms
* Working on robotics and smart systems

### **2.2 Tools and Technologies**

* AI frameworks
* Automation tools

# A Deep Research Study on Cybersecurity: Roles, Skills, Industry Demand, and Future Trends (2026–2030)

## 1. Introduction

Cybersecurity is the field of protecting computers, networks, cloud systems, applications, and data from attacks, misuse, theft, and disruption. It has become one of the most important areas in IT because modern organizations depend heavily on digital systems, and cyberattacks now affect governments, banks, hospitals, universities, startups, and ordinary users. Recent global reports show that cybersecurity demand is rising because cybercrime is becoming more sophisticated, digital transformation is expanding the attack surface, and organizations are struggling to hire enough skilled professionals. ([World Economic Forum][1])

Cybersecurity is no longer only a “technical support” function. In 2026, it is a business-critical function tied to risk management, regulation, customer trust, operational continuity, and even national security. The World Economic Forum notes that “networks and cybersecurity” are among the fastest-growing skills projected toward 2030, while labor-market data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics shows very strong job growth for information security analysts. ([World Economic Forum][2])

## 2. What Cybersecurity Means in Practice

In simple terms, cybersecurity means making sure that:

* only the right people can access systems and data,
* systems stay available and do not go down during attacks,
* information is not changed illegally,
* software and infrastructure are built and operated safely.

These goals are often described through confidentiality, integrity, and availability. In real organizations, cybersecurity includes prevention, detection, response, recovery, governance, compliance, and security awareness. The NIST NICE Framework describes cybersecurity work in a structured way and notes 52 work roles across seven categories, showing how broad the field really is. ([NIST][3])

## 3. Why Cybersecurity Is Growing So Fast

Cybersecurity is growing because digital systems are growing. Companies now use cloud platforms, remote work tools, APIs, SaaS products, mobile apps, AI systems, and connected devices. Every new technology increases convenience, but it also creates new weaknesses if not secured properly. The World Economic Forum’s recent cybersecurity outlook highlights that geopolitical tensions, supply-chain interdependence, emerging technologies, and cybercrime sophistication are making the security landscape more complex. ([World Economic Forum][1])

Threat reports from ENISA show that ransomware, data threats, availability attacks, social engineering, and supply-chain issues remain major concerns. ENISA’s threat landscape identifies threats against availability, ransomware, and threats against data among the leading categories, and later sector reports continue to show supply-chain exploitation, fraud, social engineering, and operational disruption as recurring risks. ([ENISA][4])

Cybersecurity is also growing because the financial damage is high. IBM’s 2025 Cost of a Data Breach report puts the global average breach cost at about **USD 4.4 million**, showing that weak security is expensive. The same report emphasizes that rapid AI adoption without proper governance increases risk. ([IBM][5])

## 4. Main Areas of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is not one job. It is a collection of specialized areas.

### 4.1 Network Security

This focuses on protecting routers, switches, firewalls, VPNs, traffic, and internal communications. It includes segmentation, intrusion detection, secure access, and traffic monitoring. This area remains important because attackers often move through networks after the first breach. ([ENISA][4])

### 4.2 Application Security

This focuses on building secure software and testing applications for weaknesses. It includes secure coding, code review, dependency management, API security, and penetration testing. OWASP’s Top 10 for 2025 remains a widely used reference for the most critical web application risks, and the 2025 release emphasizes modern issues such as software supply-chain failures. ([OWASP Foundation][6])

### 4.3 Cloud Security

Cloud security protects infrastructure, identities, configurations, workloads, containers, and data across platforms such as AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud. This area is growing very fast because organizations are running hybrid and multi-cloud systems, which add complexity and risk. IBM notes that breaches involving multiple environments can be especially costly. ([IBM][7])

### 4.4 Identity and Access Management (IAM)

IAM ensures the correct people and systems get the correct level of access. It includes multi-factor authentication, least privilege, role-based access, privileged access management, and identity governance. As organizations move to cloud and remote work, identity has become one of the central security layers. ([World Economic Forum][1])

### 4.5 Security Operations and Incident Response

This area monitors alerts, investigates suspicious behavior, responds to incidents, and helps organizations recover after attacks. Security Operations Centers, or SOCs, are central to this work. Because attacks happen continuously, incident response and threat detection remain among the most practical and employable cybersecurity paths. ([World Economic Forum][1])

### 4.6 Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)

GRC focuses on policies, controls, risk assessments, audits, laws, and standards. This area is important because cybersecurity is not just about stopping hackers; it is also about proving to customers, regulators, and executives that the organization manages risk responsibly. ([NIST][3])

### 4.7 Offensive Security / Ethical Hacking

This area simulates attacker behavior to find weaknesses before criminals do. It includes penetration testing, red teaming, exploit research, and adversary emulation. It is popular among students, but real industry jobs often require solid foundations in systems, networking, scripting, and reporting. ([NIST][3])

## 5. Current Threat Trends in 2026

The current threat environment is shaped by a few major trends.

First, **ransomware and extortion** remain dominant. Attackers no longer only encrypt files; they also steal data and threaten leaks, creating “multi-extortion” pressure. ENISA continues to highlight ransomware and data threats as top concerns. ([ENISA][4])

Second, **social engineering is becoming more dangerous**, especially with AI-assisted phishing, impersonation, and fake websites. ENISA’s 2025 reporting includes fraudulent websites impersonating legitimate AI tools and malware disguised as AI installers. ([ENISA][8])

Third, **software supply-chain risk** is growing. OWASP Top 10:2025 elevated software supply-chain failures as a major web security risk, reflecting industry concern that insecure dependencies, packages, and build pipelines can compromise many systems at once. ([OWASP Foundation][9])

Fourth, **AI changes both attack and defense**. Attackers use AI to improve phishing, automate reconnaissance, and scale deception. Defenders use AI to improve detection, triage, and response speed. IBM’s 2025 breach report frames this as an “AI oversight gap,” where adoption is moving faster than governance. ([IBM][5])

Fifth, **secure-by-design** is becoming a stronger expectation. CISA has been pushing secure-by-design and secure-by-default practices, including progress reporting from vendors and targeted guidance on eliminating risky software patterns such as memory-unsafe weaknesses. ([CISA][10])

## 6. Major Cybersecurity Job Roles

Cybersecurity offers many career paths, not just one.

### 6.1 SOC Analyst

A SOC analyst monitors alerts, investigates suspicious activity, escalates incidents, and uses SIEM and endpoint tools. This is one of the most common entry paths because it builds real defensive experience. The field’s job growth makes this a strong role for graduates. ([Bureau of Labor Statistics][11])

### 6.2 Security Analyst

This role is broader and may include vulnerability management, policy review, threat analysis, and coordination with IT teams. It is often suitable for people who want both technical and operational exposure. ([NIST][3])

### 6.3 Penetration Tester / Ethical Hacker

This role tests systems by thinking like an attacker. It requires technical skill, reporting ability, and strong ethics. Although many students want this role, there are generally fewer beginner jobs here than in blue-team operations. ([NIST][3])

### 6.4 Cloud Security Engineer

This role secures cloud infrastructure, IAM, storage, containers, workloads, and CI/CD pipelines. Because organizations are moving heavily into cloud and hybrid environments, this role is becoming increasingly important. ([IBM][7])

### 6.5 Application Security Engineer

This role works closely with developers to build secure code, review architecture, secure APIs, and reduce software risk early in the lifecycle. It is especially important as secure-by-design becomes more central. ([OWASP Foundation][6])

### 6.6 Incident Responder / Threat Hunter

These professionals analyze incidents deeply, investigate attacker behavior, and improve detection logic. They are critical for organizations that need mature defensive capability. ([World Economic Forum Reports][12])

### 6.7 GRC Analyst / Compliance Analyst

This role suits people who are organized, analytical, and interested in security policy, audits, controls, and regulatory alignment. It is less flashy than hacking, but very important in real business. ([NIST][3])

## 7. Skills Required in Cybersecurity

The best cybersecurity professionals combine technical skills, business understanding, and communication.

### Technical foundations

Core technical foundations include:

* networking basics,
* operating systems, especially Linux and Windows,
* scripting, often Python or Bash,
* web technologies and APIs,
* logging and monitoring concepts,
* cloud basics,
* identity and access concepts.

These foundations matter because cybersecurity tools are built on top of systems knowledge, not instead of it. The NICE Framework’s work-role model is useful because it connects tasks to the knowledge and skills expected in the field. ([NIST][3])

### Security-specific skills

Important security-specific skills include:

* incident analysis,
* vulnerability assessment,
* threat modeling,
* secure configuration,
* access control,
* SIEM and endpoint detection concepts,
* secure development awareness,
* risk assessment.

Application security awareness is especially valuable because OWASP Top 10:2025 reflects real-world web risk priorities. ([OWASP Foundation][6])

### Human and professional skills

Cybersecurity also requires:

* clear writing,
* reporting,
* teamwork,
* critical thinking,
* calm decision-making under pressure,
* ethics.

The World Economic Forum continues to highlight broader human-centered skills such as resilience, flexibility, and leadership alongside technical demand. ([World Economic Forum][13])

## 8. Industry Demand and Career Outlook

Cybersecurity is one of the strongest career areas in IT. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects **29% growth** for information security analysts from 2024 to 2034, much faster than average, with about **16,000 openings each year** on average. That is a strong signal of sustained demand. ([Bureau of Labor Statistics][11])

At the same time, the workforce shortage is still a major issue. ISC2’s recent research continues to emphasize skills gaps and hiring challenges, and World Economic Forum reporting also points to ongoing demand for network and cybersecurity skills. This means the market is favorable for people who build practical skills rather than only academic knowledge. ([ISC2][14])

For students, the key point is this: cybersecurity is not a dying field or a niche field. It is a long-term, expanding field connected to every industry that uses technology. That includes finance, healthcare, telecom, logistics, education, manufacturing, government, retail, and startups. ([World Economic Forum][1])

## 9. How the Industry Is Moving from 2026 to the Future

From 2026 onward, the industry is moving in several clear directions.

### 9.1 More AI in defense

Organizations are using AI to improve detection, alert triage, and response speed. AI is not replacing cybersecurity professionals, but it is changing workflows. People who understand both security and AI-assisted tools will have an advantage. ([IBM Mediacenter][15])

### 9.2 More focus on secure-by-design

Security is moving earlier in the lifecycle. Instead of only finding problems after release, organizations want safer defaults, safer code, safer architectures, and safer vendor practices from the beginning. CISA’s secure-by-design push reflects this direction. ([CISA][10])

### 9.3 More cloud and identity-centered security

As organizations continue to use SaaS, cloud, and hybrid systems, identity, access, and cloud configuration will stay central. Security professionals who understand IAM, cloud posture, and zero-trust principles will be more valuable. ([IBM][7])

### 9.4 More regulation and governance pressure

Boards, regulators, customers, and partners increasingly expect formal security governance, risk reporting, and assurance. That means technical security and GRC will grow together, not separately. ([World Economic Forum][1])

### 9.5 More supply-chain and third-party risk focus

Organizations now know that security is not only about their own code and servers. Vendors, libraries, packages, CI/CD tools, and service providers can all become entry points. That is why software supply-chain security is becoming a top concern. ([OWASP Foundation][9])

## 10. Best Career Path for University Undergraduates

For undergraduates, the best path is usually **not** to jump straight into advanced hacking topics without foundations. A better path is:

1. Learn networking basics, Linux, Windows, and how the web works.
2. Learn Python or Bash for automation.
3. Learn core security concepts: authentication, authorization, encryption, logging, vulnerabilities, incident response.
4. Practice hands-on using labs, CTFs, vulnerable apps, and cloud sandboxes.
5. Build a portfolio with reports, write-ups, or small security projects.
6. Choose a direction: SOC, cloud security, app security, GRC, pentesting, or threat hunting.
7. Add certifications only after basic knowledge is real.

This advice matches the industry’s practical emphasis on demonstrable skills, which NIST NICE and ISC2 both reinforce. ([NIST][3])

## 11. Common Mistakes Students Make

A common mistake is thinking cybersecurity means only ethical hacking. In reality, many jobs are in monitoring, compliance, cloud security, IAM, application security, and incident response. Another mistake is collecting certificates without hands-on practice. A third mistake is ignoring writing and communication. Security professionals must explain risk clearly to technical teams and non-technical stakeholders. The NICE Framework’s work-role approach shows that cybersecurity is task-based and skill-based, not only certificate-based. ([NIST][3])

Another mistake is ignoring software and cloud fundamentals. Modern security work increasingly overlaps with development, architecture, and cloud operations. Secure-by-design and application-security trends make this overlap even stronger. ([OWASP Foundation][6])

Cybersecurity in 2026 is one of the strongest and most future-proof IT fields. It is growing because the world is more digital, attackers are more capable, regulations are increasing, and the business cost of security failure is high. Job demand is strong, the skills gap remains real, and the field now touches every part of modern technology: cloud, software, AI, infrastructure, identity, data, and governance. ([Bureau of Labor Statistics][11])

For students, cybersecurity is a very good career choice, but success comes from strong foundations and practical work, not shortcuts. The future belongs to people who can combine technical depth, security thinking, and clear communication. # 📄 **A Deep Research Study on Information Systems Engineering (ISE): Concepts, Roles, Technologies, and Future Trends (2026–2030)**

---

## **1. Introduction**

Information Systems Engineering (ISE) is a field that focuses on designing, developing, and managing systems that collect, process, store, and distribute information within organizations.

In modern businesses, information systems are essential for:

* Decision-making
* Data management
* Business operations
* Communication

In 2026, almost every organization—from startups to large enterprises—depends on information systems such as ERP, CRM, and business intelligence tools. As data becomes more important, Information Systems Engineering plays a key role in ensuring that information is accurate, secure, and available.

---

## **2. What Information Systems Engineering Means in Practice**

In simple terms, ISE ensures that:

* Data is collected correctly
* Information is processed efficiently
* Systems support business operations
* Decision-makers get accurate insights

ISE connects:
👉 Technology + Business + Data

It includes:

* System design
* Database management
* Business process integration
* Information flow management

# 📄 **Surviving the Future IT Industry: How to Stay Relevant Across Fields with AI (2026–2030)**

---

## **1. Introduction**

The IT industry is changing rapidly due to Artificial Intelligence (AI), automation, and cloud technologies. Many traditional roles are evolving, and some low-skill jobs are being replaced by automation.

Because of this, many students and professionals fear:
👉 “Will I lose my job in the future?”

The answer is:
❌ You won’t lose your job if you adapt
✅ You will lose your job if you stay outdated

This article explains how to stay relevant across major IT fields by using AI as a tool instead of seeing it as a threat.

---

## **2. The Big Shift in IT Industry**

In the past:

* Developers wrote everything manually
* Engineers managed systems manually
* Analysts worked without AI tools

In 2026:

* AI helps write code
* AI automates tasks
* AI supports decision-making

👉 So the role is changing from:
**“Doing work manually” → “Using AI to do work faster”**

---

## **3. Key Idea to Survive**

👉 The most important rule:

💡 **Don’t compete with AI — work with AI**
